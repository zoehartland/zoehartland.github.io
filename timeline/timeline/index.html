1964-1968

The beginning of a single European market

Although the UK wasn’t yet involved, what was to become the modern EU began to take shape.

The European Community was formed in 1967, there were 6 members Belgium, France, Germany, Italy, Luxembourg and the Netherlands. The following year “freedom of movement” was introduced.

1965 -1971

Commonwealth restrictions

Between 1965 and 1971 a series of immigration changes for commonwealth citizens were introduced.

  • British Nationality Act 1965 – allowed wives of British subjects to obtain British nationality
  • In 1965 the number of employment vouchers available for Commonwealth citizens via the 1962 Commonwealth Act was reduced
  • Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1968 - commonwealth citizens could only enter the UK if they had a parent or grandparent who was a UK national.

1945-1982

Ten pound pom emigration

Emigrants relocating from the UK to Australia using the "Assisted Passage Migration Scheme" reached their peak in 1969.

Between 1945 and 1972 over 1 million “Ten pound poms” took advantage of the fact that adults could relocate cheaply and children could travel for free.

The scheme ended in 1982.

1971-1975

World unrest

War in Bangladesh led to 10 million East Bengali refugees leaving the country in 1971 and the following year, 28,000 Ugandan’s settled in the UK in two months after being expelled from their country.

The 1973 oil crisis causes the UK to enter a recession and unemployment rates fall.

Political unrest and the end of the Vietnam war in South-East Asia results in many seeking a new life in Britain in 1975. It is estimated that 83 thousand immigrants from the Commonwealth have settled in the UK since 1968.

1972-1973

Easier movement within the EC

In 1972 the European Communities Act was passed which paved the way for the UK (along with Republic of Ireland and Denmark) to join the European Community in 1973. As a result, nationals of member countries gained the right to enter and live in the UK freely. In turn, UK citizens gained the right to live and work in other EC countries.

At the same time the Immigration Act, implemented in 1973 made immigration from commonwealth countries to the UK harder.

1976

Tension in the UK and motivation to emigrate

After the mid-1970s recession Britain applied to the International Monetary Fund for a £2.3 billion loan as a result of the financial crisis. Employment rates were low and there was a level of racial tension in the UK.

Existing laws to prevent racial discrimination were ineffective and in 1976 the Race Relations Act was introduced to promote equal opportunities regardless of race, colour, nationality, ethnicity or religion.

The Canadian Immigration Act 1976 encouraged family reunification and 23,000 people left the UK for Canada this year.

1980-1986

Law changes and a growing EC

A series of new laws tighten down on eligibility for citizenship and immigration:

  • Primary purpose rule in 1980 – required proof that the primary purpose of marriage was not to gain British residency
  • British Nationality Act 1981 (implemented in 1983) meant that children born outside the UK to British overseas citizens no longer had automatic British citizenship and right to abode.

Greece joined the EC in 1981, with Portugal and Spain joining in 1986.

1985-1988

Movement around the EC became easier

The Schengen Agreement 1985 - introduced a passport control free travel area (not including the UK) for EC countries.

The Immigration Act 1988, ensured people with freedom of movement in the European Communities did not need leave to enter or remain in the UK

1989-1990

Baltic independence

The Berlin Wall was pulled down in 1989 – the first time the border between East and West Germany was open in 28 years. 1990 saw the end of Communist Governments across Eastern Europe and an enlarged and unified Germany became a EC member.

The following year Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania gained their independence.

In the UK the current Work Permits system was introduced to enable employers to recruit skilled people.

1993-1994

The single market

The Maastricht Treaty established the European Union (EU) in 1993 completing the creation of the single market. The single market allowed freedom of movement of goods services, people and money and extended to economically inactive EU nationals as well as workers.

In 1994 the European Economic Area agreement comes into force bringing together the EU member states and Iceland, Liechtenstein and Norway (European Free Trade Area , or EFTA) — in a single market.

The Channel Tunnel opened in 1994 enabling easier movement between the UK and France. The Sangatte agreement was implemented so the UK could carry out immigration controls on French territory.

1995-1999

Preparation for EU expansion and the Euro

Austria, Finland and Sweden join the European Union in 1995. 2 years later, in 1997 the UK handed back Hong Kong to China.

In 1999 the Amsterdam Treaty came into force to prepare the EU for enlargement. Member states agreed to devolve certain powers from national governments to the European Parliament, to increase co-operation and protection in areas such as immigration, civil and criminal law, foreign and security policy, health and consumer protection.

In the same year The Euro was introduced as the new “single currency” with 11 member states of the EU adopting its use.

1999-2002

Asylum seeker law change

In 2002 asylum applications to the UK peaked at over 80,000 , mainly from Africa, Asia and the Middle East. A number of processes and laws were introduced to specifically address the asylum process:

  • Immigration and Asylum Act 1999, which aimed to speed up the asylum process to combat illegal entry and strengthen powers to deal with those not entitled to remain in the UK.
  • Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002, which amended the support arrangements for asylum seekers and created provisions for detention and removal.

2004

The EU8 Accession

The EU8 (Czech Republic Estonia Hungary Latvia Lithuania Poland Slovakia and Slovenia) plus Malta and Cyprus joined the EU in 2004. Over the following years, the UK saw an immediate increase in the number of migrants coming from these countries.

The Worker Registration Scheme was introduced in 2004 to monitor incoming workers from these countries and help keep track of how the labour market was affected by this influx of migrants. This scheme ended in 2011.

2008-2009

The economic downturn

In 2008 the UK entered a double dip recession. Immigration from the EU8 had risen sharply, and unemployment was high. A points based immigration system was introduced to replace all previous work permits and entry schemes.

In 2009 the number of student visas peaked at more than 300,000 for the first time and the EU expanded further with the addition of Bulgaria and Romania (EU2). Citizens of the EU2 had employment restrictions when they first joined the EU. These were lifted in 2014.

Croatia joined in 2013 the EU as the 28th member state. In 2016 the UK voted to leave the EU and discussions have begun on what effect this will have on migration into and out of the UK in the future.
